The problem

According to the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention, men died by suicide at a rate of 3.54 percent higher than women in 2017. Depression and suicide are ranked as a leading cause of death among men, and yet they’re still far less likely to seek mental health treatment than women. In our society, a lot of men don’t want to admit they have this problem. They still see depression as a sign of weakness. There are still some outdated stereotypes as a man suffering from depression; men as a source of Strength, dominating positions of power, the hunter-gatherer, the idea that strong and silent is alluring/attractive, the "show no weakness" bravado of heroes in our media. However, this type of thinking is outdated, a relic of previous generations that doesn’t speak to the current medical understanding of mental illness. Depression, anxiety, personality disorders and schizophrenia are no more or less difficult based on whether someone has a Y chromosome in their DNA. Hence, there has never been a better time to leverage advanced technologies to create a safe space that allows men to seek – and be accepted for – help with their mental wellbeing.

UX Research Goals

Men's Talk is an encrypted mobile application, designed for middle-aged men who do not feel comfortable seeking mental health services in person to track their symptoms and open up about their struggles with a supportive community and providers without having to disclose their identity. Men's Talk will help men break through the stigma and obstacles by providing a safe and trusted platform, on which the user can simply ask questions and get advice. Combined with the biometric data (I.e. heart rate) collected from the user’s wearable devices, Men's Talk uses natural language processing to perform sentimental analysis of the user’s interactions and will generate a chart that shows the user's change of mood over a period of time. The experience can fight the stigma that real men don’t talk about their troubles by allowing them to open up to others.


Research Methodology

The main goal of the user research is to understand the users and their experience in relation to mental health struggles in general. I will conduct semi-structured interviews for the product. The main reasons for choosing semi-structured interviews over contextual inquiry is because mental health struggles are not a concrete one-time event in a predictable setting, but instead is a long-lived experience should have come across. There is no specific thing to be observed; nor is there a need to question the participants while they work in a particular environment. Hence, detailed questions about user practices, thoughts and experiences with mental health management, through a one-on-one format, would be sufficient to elicit relevant responses from the participants. Notes will be taken during and after each interview. Audio recordings will be used to assist data collection with the permission of the participants.

I employ an iterative, user-centered participatory design (UCD) approach to actively involve all stakeholders in the design process to help ensure the product meets their needs and is usable. In the cyclic process of prototyping, testing, analyzing, and refining the product, the stakeholders (users, MH professionals, UX experts) will participate during several stages of an innovation process: during the initial exploration and problem definition to help define the problem and to focus ideas for solution, and during development, to help evaluate proposed product prototype. These phases are informed by Nielsen’s Usability Design Lifecycle. The result will allow me to make appropriate modifications and confirm design concepts as the prototype matured from wireframe mock-ups into a functional application (low-fi -> mid-fi -> high-fi). I will iteratively refine process to determine that I have accurately represented the user stories about mental health struggles. Nielson's 10 Heuristics evaluation will be conducted of the mid-fi prototype by 2 recruited UX researchers to identify major usability problems in the user interface design. As the prototype takes sophisticated form and becomes highly functional, I will carry out a Rigorous Evaluation in a lab setting to measure the quality of the user's experience when interacting with the product, with the focus on learnability, memorability, comprehensibility and subjective satisfaction.

Process

I analysed the data by, firstly, annotating the interview notes with codes in the margins. Secondly, I created a card for each of the code using post-its (yellow). With these self-standing cards, I then put them into corresponding clusters, and identified 6 common themes labelled (white) as “Anonymity”, “Mental Health Services”, “Emotion Management”, “Communication”, “Friends and Family” and “Support Network” respectively. Finally, I combined these 6 themes and created 3 high-level categories labelled “Barriers & Stigma”, “Methods” and “Network” (lined paper), which summarize all of the collected data declaratively.

Summary of Affinity Diagram

"Barriers and Stigma" categorizes the participants’ perceived barriers to reaching out to mental health providers, such as insurance reimbursement and intricately confusing process, and the importance of staying anonymous when using these services. Methods categorizes the means by which the participants manage their emotional swings, including texting with friends, binge eating and exercising. And Network condenses the formal and informal social support networks that the participants rely on for stress management, such as the fellowship at church. It was surprising to find out the importance of a single-sex platform for the users when it comes to a mental health support network. To quote one participant, “I cannot share my deepest struggles relating to my family or marriage with anyone at church because my wife is part of it too” . Another participant also states, “there are some issues that only men understand like sexual problems”.

Name: Zac
Age: 38
Occupation: Consultant

Persona 1

Role: Zac experiences moderate depression symptoms every so often. He has used mental health services before but stopped, mainly due to his busy work schedule, and improved condition that no longer requires medications. Goals: To have a group to be there for him when he is in need of emotional support. He wants to speak about his issues and struggles with people anywhere anytime, especially when he is traveling for work on his own. Tasks: Have a secured and safe community network that allows him to communicate with a group that can understand and relate to his problems. Environment: He is always out of town and on the road. His constant travelling has made it difficult to have a physical and stable support network. A quote: “The airport lounge is where my emotions usually kick in. I travel a lot for work, and when I cannot see my wife and kids, I get easily consumed by all the negative thoughts and self-doubts. At times like this, I just want to know that I am not alone”



Name: Gregory

Persona 2

Heuristics Evaluation

Nielsen's 10 Heuristics evaluation was independently conducted of the mid-fi prototype by 2 UX researchers. This process specifically involves evaluators examining the interface and judging its compliance with recognized usability principles. This allows me to identify any usability issues that may arise in the user interface before it is put into wide use. The violations below were concluded by the researchers:

User Control and Freedom - severity: 4

A home screen that allows the user to access the two types of chat (group / doctor) and the “find clinic” and “self help techniques” would allow the user to easily navigate and be in control of where he is in the app. This is a critical element in a a prototype to get better understanding of the general UX of the app.

Consistency and Standard - severity: 2

“Chat with a doctor” screen where user can connect to his primary care provider directly. This is the only screen that isn’t consistent with the rest of the screen design wise - the header is of a different color (a green that does not appear in any of the other screens) and the structure of the header is also different. This jumps out because the rest of the screens are very consistent.

Error Prevention - severity: 2

Screen after selecting specific group to join that contains the channels. This screen is highly sensitive as very personal information is sent to practically strangers and yet pictures of the people appear here- I think having a quick “unsend” button can be useful for users - similar to gmail’s unsend button.

Recognition Rather than Recall - severity: 3

“Find a clinic” screen that allows users to input their mental issues and other health related details for getting suggestions for care providers. When the input inserts details - they should have rich drop down lists for different detail fields of options they can choose from rather than free text. This will allow the user to recall and think of all their health related issues.

Match Between System and Real World - severity: 4

Screen after selecting specific group to join that contains the channels. This screen (or login/ signup) doesn’t have an annonymizing function to allow users to be anonymous even partially when talking to people about very personal issues.

User Journeys

Having taken the 5 violations identified in the Heuristic evaluation into consideration, I modified the user flow. The "Find a Clinic" and "Chat with a Doctor" functions have been combined into one function ("Professional Help") to enhance the clarity of the information architecture, as well as to decrease the cognitive workload of the users. This modification was made following a discussion with the UX evaluators who expressed confusion about having to choose between the two functions– as one was uncertain whether their condition would require an in-person visit to the clinic or just a virtual chat with a provider. The modified design factors in the information filled out by the user in the "Professional Help" interface, whereby provide a suggestion about the next step.

Key Learnings

Be an interviewer, not a note-taker.

I realised the significance of really paying attention to what the participant is saying and exchangeing eye contacts with them throughout the interviews. Such gestures made the participants feel more respected, and in turn more comfortable to open up about themselves to me.

Pay attention to every interaction with the participant.

One of the challenges I faced during data collection was arranging face-to-face interviews with the participants. Most of the participants were reluctant to be interviewed in-person or to be audio recorded. While the reasons were not explicitly explored, it may imply the stigma and sensitivity around the topic of mental health that should be given further thoughts.

Diversity of thoughts.

There are things that can easily slip my mind, hence it is always beneficial to ask for the opinions of other designers. With a different education, upbrining, and character, each designer can bring a different piece of puzzle to the bigger picture.
